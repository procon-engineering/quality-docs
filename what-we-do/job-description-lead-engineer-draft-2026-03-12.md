## About the Role

The Lead Engineer owns the technical delivery of a project from design kick-off through to as-built close-out. On an offshore wind foundation electrical package, that means 60–100 documents, two or more discipline engineers, a client relationship, and a package that ends up fabricated and installed on a structure in the North Sea.

This is not a pure engineering role and not a pure project management role — it is both, weighted towards engineering. The Lead Engineer sets the technical direction, checks and approves the work of the discipline engineers, carries the client interface, and is accountable for the quality of everything that leaves Procon under their name.

Lead Engineers at Procon come from either an electrical or mechanical background. Both are valid. The role is structured to work with either — but you need to be honest about which discipline is yours and manage the other through your specialist engineers accordingly.

## Responsibilities

### 1. Client Interface
You are the primary technical contact for the client on your project:
- Attend and lead design review meetings with the client
- Manage the Technical Query (TQ) cycle — receive client and WTG manufacturer TQs, coordinate responses from discipline engineers, review and approve before issue
- Present findings, open points, and design decisions to the client in writing and in meetings
- Maintain a professional, clear, and technically credible relationship — the client judges Procon's quality through you
- Route anything with commercial or scope implications to the Engineering Director before responding to the client; you do not agree to scope changes

### 2. Project Management
Lightweight PM is part of the role — not a separate function:
- Maintain the document delivery schedule; know which documents are due, which are in review, and which are blocked
- Coordinate between the electrical engineer, mechanical engineer, and any specialist contributors (structural, SCADA, etc.) to keep the package moving
- Flag programme risks to the Engineering Director early — do not absorb schedule pressure into the technical work
- Track open points and findings; ensure nothing falls between the cracks between disciplines or between Procon and external parties
- Coordinate with procurement on technical inputs they need — specifications, data sheets, review of returned vendor documentation

### 3. Checking and Technical Review
Cross-document checking is a core function of the Lead Engineer — not a secondary task:
- Review every document produced by the discipline engineers for technical correctness, internal consistency, and compliance with project requirements before it is issued to the client
- Check cross-document consistency — that the cable list agrees with the schematic, the load list agrees with the power study, the tagging schedule is complete against the equipment list, the design report reflects what the drawings actually show
- Maintain a structured finding register; record every issue with severity, owner, and resolution; present to the Engineering Director and client at agreed milestones
- Apply checking depth proportional to risk — a major design document gets more scrutiny than a revision that fixes a typo; but you own the decision about what level of scrutiny is appropriate
- In your primary discipline, perform the technical check yourself. In your secondary discipline, direct your specialist engineer to perform the deep check — but you must be able to understand and stand behind their findings when presenting to the client

### 4. Overall Quality Responsibility
The quality of the project package is yours:
- All documents issued under your project must meet the Procon quality standard — correct document IDs, complete reference tables, clean revision histories, DMS version alignment
- Ensure the package is internally consistent as a whole — not just that each document is individually acceptable, but that the package tells a coherent technical story
- Identify systemic issues — where the same error appears across multiple documents, the root cause is usually a template or process problem, not just an individual error; flag these for corrective action
- Sign off the package before client submission; do not issue under time pressure if the quality is not there — escalate the schedule conflict to the Engineering Director instead

### 5. Technical Leadership
Set the technical direction for the project:
- Make technical decisions within the project scope — design choices, interface definitions, scope boundary calls
- Document decisions — if a technical choice is made, write it down with the reasoning; undocumented decisions become disputes
- Identify and flag risks early — scope interfaces with other contractors, open TQs, unresolved design questions; do not sit on blockers
- Mentor the discipline engineers on project-specific requirements and quality standards

## Decision Authority

### You can decide independently:
- Technical design decisions within the established project scope and client specification
- Scope boundary calls at the discipline level (e.g. confirming what is Procon scope vs WTG manufacturer scope vs IAC contractor scope)
- Severity classification of findings and whether a finding is a genuine non-conformance or a not-a-finding
- Whether a document revision is minor (checker/approver waiver acceptable) or major (full review required)
- Hold a document from issue if quality is not acceptable

### You decide, then inform the Engineering Director:
- A TQ response that commits to a design change — even a small one
- A scope boundary decision that has not been formally agreed with the client
- A finding that the client is likely to escalate commercially
- A programme delay or risk that affects the project delivery date

### You must escalate before acting:
- Any change to the project scope, contract, or commercial terms — this is Engineering Director territory, not Lead Engineer
- A serious quality non-conformance that may require client notification
- A technical risk that you cannot resolve within the project team
- Anything that involves direct commitment to the client outside of a pre-agreed technical position

## Your Technical Profile

Lead Engineers at Procon come from one of two backgrounds. Both are needed; neither can cover the full package alone without specialist support. Be clear about which you are — and manage the other discipline accordingly.

### If your background is electrical:
You will personally perform the deep technical review of:
- LV schematics, cable lists, load lists, studies (earthing, illumination, power), tagging schedules, SCADA documents
- Standards compliance (IEC 61892, IEC 60204-1, IEC 60332, RDS-PP)
- Cross-document electrical consistency

For the mechanical scope, you are expected to:
- Understand the deliverables and what they are for — cable support structures, equipment frames, sub-assembly drawings, BOMs
- Identify obvious issues — missing HDPE isolation in a BOM, an incorrect material grade, a BOM that doesn't match the drawing
- Recognise when something looks wrong and engage the mechanical engineer or structural specialist to verify
- Own the finding, even if the specialist identified it — it is your package

### If your background is mechanical:
You will personally perform the deep technical review of:
- Cable support drawings, equipment frames, cable containment routing, dropper system, sub-assembly drawings and BOMs
- Material compliance (316L, EN AW 5083, galvanic isolation), fabrication readiness, structural handoff quality
- Cross-document mechanical consistency

For the electrical scope, you are expected to:
- Understand the deliverables — schematics, cable lists, load lists, studies — and what they should contain
- Identify obvious issues — incomplete reference tables, missing equipment coverage, ALL vs NSCA corrections
- Recognise when a calculation or standard reference looks wrong and engage the electrical engineer to verify
- Own the finding, even if the specialist identified it — it is your package

**In both cases:** the Lead Engineer is not a passive relay between specialists and the client. You must understand the findings well enough to explain them, defend them, and judge whether they are resolved.

## Typical Tasks

### In a design phase week:
- Review two documents produced by the electrical engineer — check cross-document consistency against the cable list and equipment list; record findings in the project register
- Chair a design coordination meeting between the electrical and mechanical engineers; resolve a routing conflict between cable containment and a bracket position
- Review a TQ from the WTG manufacturer about signal cable specification; coordinate the technical response with the electrical engineer; approve before issue
- Present the open points register to the Engineering Director; flag a TQ that has been unanswered by the client for three weeks

### In a procurement and fabrication phase week:
- Review a vendor-returned FECB schematic; confirm the electrical engineer's deviation mark-up is complete; approve for return to procurement
- Respond to a client question about material specification for the HV cable support structure; confirm 316L and cite the project specification
- Check the current finding register; confirm that all Medium and Major findings from the last client review have a documented resolution or action owner
- Review the latest DMS upload — confirm document versions are consistent with the title block versions

### In an installation and commissioning phase week:
- Review a site RFI (Request for Information) about a bracket fit-up issue; coordinate with the mechanical engineer; issue a written response
- Review and close out completed installation check sheets; confirm all required measurements are recorded and within tolerance
- Compile and issue the as-built document register to the client
- Conduct a close-out review of all findings — confirm which are closed, which are accepted deviations, and which require a future revision

## What Good Looks Like

- The client trusts your technical judgement — they may challenge findings, but they do not doubt the quality of the review
- The package is consistent — not just individually correct documents but a coherent whole
- Open points are tracked and resolved; nothing is forgotten because it was uncomfortable
- The discipline engineers know what is expected of them and work efficiently within a well-defined scope
- When you hand a document to a client, you have read it — not just signed it

## What We Are Looking For

**Experience:**
- Minimum 5 years in offshore, marine, or industrial EPC engineering, with at least 2 in a project lead or senior engineer role
- Demonstrable experience delivering a multi-document engineering package to a client — not just contributing to one
- Direct client communication experience — design reviews, TQ management, formal correspondence
- Background in either LV electrical (IEC 61892 series, schematics, cable systems) or structural/mechanical (offshore fabrication, material selection, drawing packages) — see Technical Profile above

**Tools — required:**
- Proficiency in the primary discipline tools (EPLAN and ETAP for electrical; SolidWorks/AutoCAD for mechanical)
- **Microsoft Excel and Word** — the project record and most deliverables live here; you must be able to work in them efficiently, not just open files
- Document management systems (DMS) — experience managing document registers and version control in a formal DMS environment

**Tools — advantageous:**
- **Simple scripting or programming** (Python, VBA, or similar) — for processing finding registers, cross-checking document lists, or automating consistency checks across large document sets
- Familiarity with project management tools (Monday.com or equivalent) for tracking open points and check items

**AI tools:**
Procon actively uses AI tools to improve engineering quality and throughput. As Lead Engineer, you set the standard for how AI is used on your project:
- Use AI to perform systematic cross-document consistency checks across the full package — catching issues that manual review at pace would miss
- Use AI to maintain the finding register, draft client correspondence, and process structured data (equipment lists, cable schedules)
- Set expectations for the discipline engineers on responsible AI use — verify output, own the result, never issue AI-generated content without review
- Procon's AI tooling is project-configured; you will be onboarded to the specific tools and workflows

**Mindset:**
- Owns the outcome, not just the process — if something is wrong in the package, it is your problem regardless of who produced it
- Comfortable making decisions under incomplete information — and documenting the basis for those decisions
- Direct with the client and with the team — problems raised early are solved cheaply; problems raised late are expensive
- Treats AI as a force multiplier — not a replacement for engineering judgement, but a tool that extends what a small team can deliver at quality
