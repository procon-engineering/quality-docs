## About the Role

The Engineering Director is the top of the engineering function at Procon. You set the technical standards the team works to, approve the documents the team produces, own the client relationships at director level, and are accountable to the CEO for the quality, capacity, and direction of engineering delivery.

This role spans three domains simultaneously: technical authority (you are the final approver and the escalation point for all engineering decisions), people and organisation (you build and develop the engineering team), and strategic direction (you define how Procon engineers — the processes, tools, and standards that govern all projects). The engineering charter is your primary governance instrument.

It is a role for someone who has done the engineering, understands what the team is dealing with at every level, and has the seniority and credibility to make final calls and stand behind them.

## Responsibilities

### 1. Document Approval Authority
You are the final signatory on Procon's engineering deliverables:
- Approve the engineering package before formal issue to the client — this is the quality gate that the entire review process leads to
- Your signature means the document meets Procon's engineering standard; it is not a rubber stamp
- Maintain awareness of what is in the package — not line-by-line, but enough to make informed approval decisions and respond to client challenges
- Where a document has been checked by a Lead Engineer and the Lead Engineer recommends approval, your review is focused on the finding register and any unresolved major issues — not repeating the detailed check

### 2. Technical Governance — Engineering Charter
The engineering charter is your primary governance tool for the engineering function:
- Own, maintain, and evolve the engineering charter — it defines how Procon engineers, at what quality level, and to what standards
- Enforce the charter across all projects and all Lead Engineers; consistency is a quality obligation, not a preference
- Resolve ambiguities and edge cases in the charter — when a Lead Engineer escalates a question about checking requirements, approval levels, or scope boundaries, your answer becomes policy
- Drive continuous improvement: lessons from completed projects feed back into the charter; what was learned on NSC A should be reflected in the process before NSC B finishes

### 3. Client Relationships at Director Level
The Lead Engineer manages the day-to-day client interface. You manage the relationship:
- Senior point of contact for the client when issues escalate beyond the project level — non-conformances, commercial disputes, scope disagreements
- Represent Procon at client director-level meetings; set the tone for the technical relationship
- Make commitments on Procon's behalf only within the agreed contract scope; route anything outside that to the CEO
- Build relationships with client technical directors and engineering managers — these relationships are assets that outlast individual projects

### 4. People and Organisation
Build and maintain the engineering team that delivers the work:
- Hire engineers at all levels — assess technical competence and cultural fit; use the job descriptions as the baseline for what each role requires
- Assign engineers to projects; balance individual development with project needs
- Performance management — set expectations, give direct feedback, act on underperformance
- Develop Lead Engineers specifically — they carry the most responsibility per head in the function; invest in them
- Build capacity ahead of demand — an engineering function that is fully stretched has no quality margin

### 5. Budget and Resource Planning
Own the engineering cost base and the capacity to deliver against the project pipeline:
- **Forward resource planning** — maintain a rolling view of engineering demand across all active and upcoming projects; map required disciplines, seniority levels, and timing against available headcount; flag capacity gaps to the CEO before they become delivery risks
- **Project assignment** — decide which Lead Engineer goes to which project; balance continuity (same engineer across project phases) against development and workload; manage transitions when projects overlap or engineers rotate
- **Capacity ahead of demand** — engineering cannot hire at the speed projects are won; build the team before it is needed, not after; work with the CEO on headcount forecasts linked to the project pipeline
- **Build-vs-subcontract decisions** — when specialist capability is needed (structural analysis, SCADA, specialist studies) or when demand temporarily exceeds capacity, decide whether to hire permanently, use a contractor, or subcontract the scope; each has cost, quality, and knowledge-retention implications
- **Budget management** — set and manage the engineering department budget: headcount, tools, training, subcontractors; monitor project engineering cost against budget; flag overruns to the CEO early
- **Bid input** — provide engineering hours and risk assessment for project bids; your estimate is the number the project budget is built on; be honest about what delivery actually requires

### 6. Innovation and Tools
Drive how Procon engineering evolves:
- Identify and adopt tools and processes that improve engineering quality and throughput — AI-assisted document review, automated consistency checking, structured knowledge bases
- Set the standard for responsible AI use across the engineering function — AI is a force multiplier, not a quality substitute; the engineer owns the output
- Evaluate new standards, industry developments, and competitive practice; decide what Procon adopts
- Create the conditions where engineers can innovate within their projects — the framework should enable, not just constrain

## Decision Authority

### You decide:
- Final approval of all engineering deliverables before client issue
- Acceptance or rejection of major technical deviations from standard or client specification
- Engineering headcount — hiring, assignment, and if necessary, exit
- Engineering tools and process standards — what the team uses and how
- Technical position on disputed client TQs where the Lead Engineer has escalated
- Whether a finding is significant enough to trigger a formal notification to the client
- Engineering department budget allocation

### You decide in consultation with the CEO:
- Scope changes to existing contracts — any engineering commitment that changes cost or programme
- New project bids — engineering hours and technical risk assessment
- Strategic capability investments (new disciplines, major tools, significant subcontractor relationships)

### You do not decide alone:
- Commercial terms with clients — Engineering Director is the technical authority; commercial terms involve the CEO
- Contracts with new subcontractors or suppliers — procurement and legal are involved
- Company-level strategic direction — that is the CEO's domain; Engineering Director inputs but does not own

## Typical Activities

### On a live project:
- Review the finding register at a project milestone; make final calls on findings the Lead Engineer has escalated for a decision — severity, acceptance, or required corrective action
- Attend a client design review alongside the Lead Engineer; provide director-level assurance on quality and programme; take ownership of any issue the Lead Engineer cannot resolve in the meeting
- Respond to a Lead Engineer's escalation on a scope boundary question — make the call, document it, ensure the Lead Engineer applies it consistently

### On engineering process:
- Review lessons learned from a completed project; identify which lessons are project-specific and which should be incorporated into the engineering charter
- Decide whether a new AI tool proposed by the team should be adopted — assess quality risk, security, and fit with existing process
- Review a new project's document plan before design starts; confirm scope, checking levels, and approval path are defined

### On people:
- Conduct a quarterly review with each Lead Engineer — workload, quality of recent deliverables, development needs
- Interview candidates for an Electrical Engineer role; assess technical competence using the job description as the standard
- Address a quality issue on a project where the Lead Engineer has not caught a systematic error — have a direct conversation about checking standards and expectations

### On budget:
- Review monthly utilisation across the engineering team; identify where capacity is tight and where demand is growing
- Provide engineering hours input to a bid for a new project; confirm the scope assumptions with the Lead Engineer before the number goes to the CEO

## What Good Looks Like

- Every document that leaves Procon under your approval is technically sound — you can defend any of them in a client meeting
- The engineering team knows exactly what standard they are working to, because the charter is clear and you enforce it consistently
- Lead Engineers escalate the right things — not everything, not nothing; the escalation threshold is well-calibrated because you have made your expectations clear
- The client trusts Procon at director level — they may push back on findings, but they do not question the integrity of the review process
- The team is developing — engineers who joined as junior are growing; Lead Engineers are getting better at their jobs; the function is stronger each year than the year before

## What We Are Looking For

**Experience:**
- 10+ years in offshore, marine, or industrial EPC engineering, with significant time in a senior engineering or technical leadership role
- Direct experience delivering engineering packages to major energy or infrastructure clients — you have been the technical responsible, not just a contributor
- People management experience — hiring, developing, and if necessary, managing out
- Budget responsibility — you have owned a cost centre or equivalent
- Background in either LV electrical or structural/mechanical engineering (the same discipline split as the Lead Engineer); deep expertise in one, working knowledge of the other

**The engineering charter:**
The engineering charter is the operating standard for the function. You are expected to understand it, apply it, and improve it. If you come from outside Procon, you will be expected to internalise it quickly — it is not optional reading.

**AI tools:**
You set the standard for AI use across the engineering function. That means you need to understand what the tools can and cannot do — not to use them yourself in detail, but to:
- Make informed decisions about adoption, risk, and process integration
- Set expectations for quality standards when AI is used in engineering deliverables
- Recognise when AI-assisted work has been reviewed and owned versus when it has been passed through uncritically
Procon is committed to being an early and effective adopter of AI in engineering practice. The Engineering Director is the person who makes that real, not just aspirational.

**Mindset:**
- Final accountability is yours — if the package is wrong, that is an Engineering Director problem, regardless of where in the process the error occurred
- Direct and consistent — the team needs to know where the bar is; ambiguity about standards is a quality risk
- Builds others up — the best outcome is a team that needs less of your time because they are getting better, not more
- Long view — process investments (charter, knowledge base, AI tools) pay back over years; resist the temptation to sacrifice them under short-term project pressure
